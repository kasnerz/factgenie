In this task, you will annotate claims about a table. In each example you will see the table title, the table and a short claim itself. For each example, you will see data on the left side and the corresponding generated textual claim on the right side. Your task is to annotate in the text with respect to the data.

There are four types of annotations that you can mark in the generated text:

  1. Incorrect fact: The fact in the text contradicts the data.
  2. Uncheckable : The fact in the text cannot be checked given the data in the table. It may be true, but it is not commonly known all over the world by most of the people.
  3. Interesting: The fact in the text is interesting for the expected audience.
  4. Obvious : The fact is boring, considered common knowledge, irrelevant, or repetitive. The opposite of interesting.

Annotate the text by dragging your mouse over the text, highlighting the text span. Once you annotated or reviewed the text for all the categories, click the âœ… Mark example as complete button. You can still update the annotation later. You will be able to submit the annotations once they are all are marked as complete.

To switch between error categories, repeatedly click on the highlighted text (the last click removes the highlight). Note that highlighting from the right to left can work better for longer spans.

  • Each error span should include all the words related to the error (but nothing else).
  • If you think the fact is probably true, but cannot be derived from the data, mark it as not checkable.
  • If you are not really sure if the fact should be marked as an error, leave it unmarked.

You can encounter the following kinds of input tables:
  • Tables about a sport event, season, league, etc.
  • Tables about a music album, artist, etc.
  • Tables about elections.
  • General tables with title like list of programming syndicated by clear channel,
